### Mildenberger (2017) - Beliefs about Climate Beliefs: The Importance of Second-Order Opinions for Climate Politics

DOI: `10.1017/S0007123417000321`

## Paper Story

The paper starts from a coordination problem in climate politics: people may hesitate to support climate action when they misread what others believe. Mildenberger and Tingley examine `second-order climate beliefs` across mass publics, political elites, and intellectual elites in the United States and China. They show that these beliefs are marked by `egocentric bias` and `global underestimation of pro-climate positions`, while beliefs about others' rationales are much more shared. They then show experimentally that updating `second-order climate beliefs` can increase support for climate cooperation. The paper concludes that biased `second-order beliefs` are an important part of explaining `climate policy inaction`.

## Verbatim Abstract
> [!quote]
> When political action entails individual costs but group-contingent benefits, political participation may depend on an individual's perceptions of others' beliefs; yet, detailed empirical attention to these second-order beliefs - beliefs about the beliefs of others - remains rare. We offer the first comprehensive examination of the distribution and content of second-order climate beliefs in the United States and China, drawing from six new opinion surveys of mass publics, political elites, and intellectual elites. We demonstrate that all classes of political actors have second-order beliefs characterized by egocentric bias and global underestimation of pro-climate positions. We then demonstrate experimentally that individual support for pro-climate policies increases after respondents update their second-order beliefs. We conclude that scholars should focus more closely on second-order beliefs as a key factor shaping climate policy inaction and that scholars can use the climate case to extend their understanding of second-order beliefs more broadly.

## Problem

1. Broader problem
- When political action has individual costs but group-contingent benefits, participation may depend on people's perceptions of others' beliefs.

2. Specific problem in this paper
- Detailed empirical attention to `second-order beliefs`, especially `second-order climate beliefs`, remains rare.

3. Why this problem matters
- If second-order climate beliefs are misperceived, they may shape support for pro-climate policies and contribute to `climate policy inaction`.

## Research Questions

1. Overall research question(s)
- How are `second-order climate beliefs` distributed across mass publics, political elites, and intellectual elites in the United States and China, and why do they matter for climate politics?

2. Within-country second-order climate beliefs
- How do American and Chinese publics perceive the distribution of climate beliefs and climate policy support within their own countries?

3. Between-country second-order climate beliefs
- How do American and Chinese publics perceive the distribution of climate beliefs in the other country's population?

4. Elite second-order climate beliefs
- Does the `egocentric bias` found among mass publics also extend to intellectual elites and political elites?

5. Experimental update of second-order beliefs
- Does updating respondents' `second-order climate beliefs` increase support for collective climate action?

6. Rationales ascribed to the beliefs of others
- What rationales do individuals ascribe to the climate beliefs of others?

## Hypotheses

1. Overall hypotheses
- No explicit hypotheses stated.

2. Survey-specific hypotheses
- No explicit hypotheses stated for the survey components or the experiment.

## Study Setup

1. Overall empirical setup
- Participants: six new opinion surveys of mass publics, political elites, and intellectual elites in the United States and China.
- Design: descriptive surveys plus an embedded survey experiment.
- Key variables: `second-order climate beliefs`, `first-order climate beliefs`, and support for `pro-climate policies`.
- Manipulations/measures: survey questions on respondents' own climate beliefs, beliefs about others' climate beliefs, and an experimental update of `second-order climate beliefs`.
- What this paper tests: the distribution and content of `second-order climate beliefs` and whether updating them shifts support for climate action.

2. Survey components

| Component | Participants | Design | What this component tests |

| March 2014 US population survey | US population, SSI, n=1,815 | nationally representative survey | within-country `second-order climate beliefs` in the United States |
| May 2014 MTurk survey | US MTurk workers, n=1,131 | non-representative survey | US beliefs about Chinese climate beliefs and cross-national comparison |
| February 2015 Chinese population survey | Chinese population, SSI, n=1,659 | national survey | within-country `second-order climate beliefs` in China |
| March 2015 US population survey | US population, SSI, n=2,073 | nationally representative survey with embedded survey experiment | updated US estimates and the experimental update of `second-order climate beliefs` |
| March 2015 TRIP Snap Poll | IR scholars, n=1,054 | elite survey | `second-order climate beliefs` among intellectual elites and beliefs about compliance with the US-China Climate Accord |

## Findings

### Within-country second-order climate beliefs
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether US and Chinese publics accurately estimated the distribution of climate beliefs in their own countries. What was found: both publics underestimated the true fraction of their own population holding `pro-climate positions`. Effect status: supported.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether `second-order climate beliefs` showed `egocentric bias` within the United States and China. What was found: respondents who personally held more pro-climate beliefs estimated more public agreement than respondents who personally disagreed. Effect status: supported.
- Null findings: none explicitly stated for this component.

### Between-country second-order climate beliefs
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether US and Chinese publics accurately estimated climate beliefs in the other country. What was found: the US public underestimated the fraction of the Chinese public with `pro-climate beliefs and preferences`, and the Chinese public did the same for the US public. Effect status: supported.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether `egocentric bias` also shaped estimates of foreign publics. What was found: there was less evidence of `egocentric bias` when estimating the distribution of beliefs among foreign publics. Effect status: mixed.
- Null findings: no explicit null effect stated beyond the weaker evidence for `egocentric bias` in foreign-public estimates.

### Elite second-order climate beliefs
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether `egocentric bias` and underestimation also appeared among intellectual elites and political elites. What was found: surveys of International Relations scholars and US congressional staffers replicated the pattern of `egocentric bias` and `global underestimation`. Effect status: supported.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether elite underestimation had a specific climate-accord pattern. What was found: among IR scholars, underestimation of Chinese support appeared linked to beliefs that the US would not, or should not, comply with the US-China Climate Accord. Effect status: supported.
- Null findings: none explicitly stated for this component.

### Experimental update of second-order beliefs
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether updating US respondents with the true distribution of Chinese climate beliefs changed support for a global climate treaty. What was found: the treatment increased support for the treaty by `0.35` on the `0-10 compliance scale`. Effect status: supported.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether the treatment effect operated through `second-order beliefs` about Chinese compliance. What was found: nearly `40%` of the average treatment effect was attributed to changes in `second-order beliefs` about Chinese compliance expectations. Effect status: supported.
- Null findings: the average treatment effect was smaller among conservatives, but no explicit null finding was stated in the cited results summary.

### Rationales ascribed to the beliefs of others
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether people with different climate beliefs ascribed different rationales to others' climate beliefs. What was found: differences in the rationales offered by those who believe climate change is happening and those who do not were substantively small and, in all but one case, statistically insignificant. Effect status: mixed.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether any rationale difference was significant. What was found: the one significant effect was that those thinking climate change is happening were more likely to say someone would use a science-based justification for why climate change is happening. Effect status: supported.
- Null findings: aside from that one effect, the differences across respondents were statistically insignificant, including the political-actor prompt described in the results summary.

## Meaning of Findings

### Within-country second-order climate beliefs
- Finding 1: Result: both publics underestimated the true fraction of their own population holding `pro-climate positions`. Meaning: people misread their own public as less pro-climate than it actually is.
- Finding 2: Result: respondents who personally held more pro-climate beliefs estimated more public agreement than respondents who personally disagreed. Meaning: `second-order climate beliefs` are shaped by respondents' own `first-order climate beliefs`.
- Null findings: Result: no explicit null finding was stated for this component. Meaning: the paper presents this component mainly as consistent evidence of underestimation and `egocentric bias`.

### Between-country second-order climate beliefs
- Finding 1: Result: US and Chinese publics underestimated pro-climate beliefs in the other country. Meaning: misperception extends across countries, not only within national publics.
- Finding 2: Result: there was less evidence of `egocentric bias` for foreign-public estimates. Meaning: people project their own beliefs more strongly onto their own public than onto foreign publics.
- Null findings: Result: no explicit null effect was stated beyond weaker `egocentric bias`. Meaning: the main cross-national pattern is still underestimation rather than accuracy.

### Elite second-order climate beliefs
- Finding 1: Result: intellectual elites and political elites also showed `egocentric bias` and `global underestimation`. Meaning: these biases are not limited to mass publics.
- Finding 2: Result: among IR scholars, underestimation of Chinese support was linked to beliefs about US compliance with the US-China Climate Accord. Meaning: elite second-order beliefs can connect climate beliefs to expectations about international cooperation.
- Null findings: Result: no explicit null finding was stated for this component. Meaning: the paper treats the elite results as corroboration of the broader pattern.

### Experimental update of second-order beliefs
- Finding 1: Result: updating respondents with the true distribution of Chinese climate beliefs increased support for a global climate treaty. Meaning: changing `second-order climate beliefs` can shift climate policy preferences.
- Finding 2: Result: nearly `40%` of the treatment effect was attributed to changes in `second-order beliefs` about Chinese compliance expectations. Meaning: part of the policy-support effect operated through updated beliefs about what others would do.
- Null findings: Result: no explicit null finding was stated for the main treatment effect. Meaning: the paper presents the experiment as evidence that `second-order beliefs` are politically consequential.

### Rationales ascribed to the beliefs of others
- Finding 1: Result: differences in ascribed rationales were usually small and statistically insignificant. Meaning: people with different climate beliefs still share fairly similar views about why others hold their positions.
- Finding 2: Result: one significant effect showed greater use of a science-based justification among those who believed climate change is happening. Meaning: the content of belief rationales shows much less divergence than the perceived distribution of climate beliefs.
- Null findings: Result: most rationale differences were statistically insignificant, including the political-actor prompt. Meaning: the paper treats the rationales domain as closer to common knowledge than the distribution of beliefs.

## Discussion

1. Theoretical contribution
- The paper argues that `second-order beliefs` are a key factor in climate politics because they shape how people perceive the distribution of climate views and the prospects for coordinated action.
- It extends work on `second-order beliefs` by showing `egocentric bias` and `global underestimation of pro-climate positions` across mass publics, political elites, and intellectual elites.
- It also distinguishes between the distribution of climate beliefs and the content of climate rationales, showing more divergence in the former than in the latter.

2. Practical implications
- Misperceptions about how pro-climate others are may reinforce `climate policy inaction`.
- Correcting `second-order climate beliefs` may increase support for collective climate action, including support for international climate cooperation.

3. Limitations
- The paper relies on survey evidence and an embedded survey experiment rather than observed long-run political behavior.
- The elite samples are narrower than the mass-public surveys, so those results should be interpreted as corroborating evidence rather than full population estimates.

## Conclusion

1. Main conclusion
- `Second-order climate beliefs` are systematically biased, matter for climate policy support, and should be treated as an important part of explaining `climate policy inaction`.

### Geiger (2016) - Climate of silence: Pluralistic ignorance as a barrier to climate change discussion

DOI: `10.1016/j.jenvp.2016.05.002`

## Paper Story
- The paper starts from the puzzle that climate change is socially important but rarely discussed in everyday conversation. It tests whether `pluralistic ignorance` about others' opinions creates `self-silencing`, first by examining naturally occurring perceptions among undergraduates and then by experimentally manipulating perceived agreement and disagreement. Across both studies, perceived disagreement reduces willingness to discuss climate change, and this pattern is explained mainly by expectations of appearing less `competent`, not less `warm`. The paper concludes that correcting `pluralistic ignorance` can remove a barrier to climate change discussion, especially among those who are already concerned.

## Verbatim Abstract
> [!quote]
> Despite the importance of interpersonal public communication about climate change, most citizens rarely discuss the topic. In two studies, we find that inaccurate perceptions of others' opinions (i.e. pluralistic ignorance) contribute to self-silencing among those concerned about climate change. Study 1 illustrates that those who are aware of others' concern about climate change report greater willingness to discuss the issue than those with inaccurate perceptions of others' opinions. Study 2 demonstrates that correcting pluralistic ignorance increases concerned participants' willingness to discuss climate change. In both studies, pluralistic ignorance leads to self-silencing because perceptions that others do not share one's opinion are associated with expecting to be perceived as less competent in a conversation about climate change. In contrast to previous research on confronting prejudice, in the present research expectations about being disliked did not explain self-silencing. We discuss the implications for self-silencing and promoting interpersonal communication about climate change.

## Key Terms

### 1. Core constructs

| Term | Very short definition in this paper |
| --- | --- |
| pluralistic ignorance | inaccurate perceptions of others' opinions about climate change |
| self-silencing | reduced willingness to discuss climate change |
| interpersonal public communication about climate change | discussion of climate change with other people |
| climate change discussion | discussion of climate change as the focal social behavior in the paper |

### 2. Study-specific terms

#### Study 1

| Term | Very short definition in this paper |
| --- | --- |
| doubtful | perceiving that most undergraduates doubt climate change |
| disengaged | perceiving that most undergraduates are not engaged with climate change |
| polarized | perceiving that undergraduates are split in their opinions |
| American public | a more abstract and distant reference group used for comparison |

#### Study 2

| Term | Very short definition in this paper |
| --- | --- |
| others' alleged opinions about climate change | experimentally provided information about what others think about climate change |
| opinion congruence | the degree to which one's opinion matches perceived opinions of others |
| concern increasing (vs. decreasing) | information that concern is becoming more versus less like one's own opinion |

## Problem

1. Broader problem
- `interpersonal public communication about climate change` can increase understanding, engagement, and consensus, yet such discussion is uncommon and climate change silence is widespread.

2. Specific problem in this paper
- The paper examines whether `pluralistic ignorance` about others' opinions contributes to `self-silencing` in climate change discussion and whether correcting that `pluralistic ignorance` can remove this barrier.

3. Why this problem matters
- `pluralistic ignorance` can reduce willingness to share one's opinion on climate change, whereas correcting it can reverse these effects.

## Research Questions

1. Overall research question(s)
- Does `pluralistic ignorance` about others' opinions contribute to `self-silencing` in `interpersonal public communication about climate change`, and can correcting that `pluralistic ignorance` increase willingness to discuss climate change?

2. Study 1 research question(s)
- Among undergraduates who do not doubt the existence of climate change, do perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions predict silence on climate change?
- Are perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions, relative to perceptions of the `American public`, related to willingness to discuss climate change and anticipated evaluations by others?

3. Study 2 research question(s)
- Does experimentally manipulating perceptions of others' opinions affect willingness to discuss climate change?
- Do the same opinion cues provide inverse effects for those who doubt versus those who are concerned about climate change?
- Do perceptions that concern about climate change is increasing versus decreasing affect willingness to discuss climate change?

## Hypotheses

1. Overall hypotheses
- `direct effect hypothesis`: Participants will be less willing to talk about climate change when they perceive that their opinions are in the minority `(vs. the majority)`.
- `mediation hypothesis`: Individuals' hesitation to discuss climate change in situations when they perceive their opinions are in the minority `(vs. the majority)` will be partly explained by expectations of appearing less warm in the conversation.
- `mediation hypothesis`: Individuals' hesitation to discuss climate change in situations when they perceive their opinions are in the minority `(vs. the majority)` will be partly explained by expectations of appearing less competent in the conversation.
- `null/alternative explanations considered`: No explicit null/alternative explanations stated.

2. Study 1 hypotheses
- No explicit hypotheses stated.

3. Study 2 hypotheses
- `moderation hypothesis`: the effects of both experimental manipulations would be moderated by personal opinions about climate change.
- `null/alternative explanations considered`: No explicit null/alternative explanations stated.

## Study Setup

### Study 1
- Participants: `305 undergraduate students` enrolled in introductory psychology classes at Pennsylvania State University; recruitment was based on pre-screening answers, and those who indicated they were `Doubtful` or a `Nonbeliever` were not recruited.
- Design: `not explicitly stated`; a follow-up survey study using pre-screening-based opinion-perception groups and an imagined discussion scenario.
- Key variables: perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions, perceptions of the `American public`'s opinions, `willingness to discuss climate change`, and anticipated evaluations of appearing `warm` and `competent`.
- Manipulations/measures: participants completed an online survey about an imagined group discussion situation; the primary predictor variable was the pre-screening category of perceptions of other students' opinions, and the study measured anticipated evaluations and willingness to discuss climate change.
- What this study tests: whether `pluralistic ignorance` predicts `self-silencing` among undergraduates who do not doubt climate change, and whether anticipated evaluations help explain that relationship.

### Study 2
- Participants: `194 students` recruited from Pennsylvania State University introductory psychology classes; the sample included people with a full range of beliefs about climate change.
- Design: experimental `2 x 2` design manipulating `others' alleged opinions about climate change` and whether `concern` was `increasing` versus `decreasing`, with `personal opinions about climate change` as a moderator.
- Key variables: `others' alleged opinions about climate change`, changes in others' alleged opinions over time, `personal opinions about climate change`, `willingness to discuss climate change`, and anticipated evaluations of appearing `warm` and `competent`.
- Manipulations/measures: participants first reported their own concern about climate change, then received classroom clicker feedback about others' opinions and changes over time; the study measured willingness to participate in a group discussion and anticipated evaluations by others.
- What this study tests: whether experimentally correcting or reinforcing `pluralistic ignorance` changes willingness to discuss climate change, whether this depends on participants' own opinions, and whether expectations of appearing `warm` and `competent` account for the effect.

## Findings

### Study 1
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions predicted `willingness to discuss climate change`, and whether the same was true for perceptions of the `American public`. What was found: perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions were related to willingness to discuss climate change; those who believed most undergraduates were `doubtful` were less willing to discuss than those with accurate perceptions that most were concerned, whereas the `disengaged` and `polarized` groups were not statistically less willing. Effect status: mixed.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions predicted anticipated evaluations and whether `warm` and `competent` mediated the relation with willingness to discuss climate change. What was found: perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions were associated with expectations of appearing `competent`, but not `warm`; the indirect effect through appearing `competent` was significant, whereas the indirect effect through appearing `warm` was not. Effect status: mixed.
- Null findings: perceptions of the `American public`'s opinions were not related to willingness to discuss climate change or to expectations of appearing `competent` or `warm`; the `disengaged` and `polarized` perception groups were not statistically less willing to discuss climate change than those with accurate perceptions.

### Study 2
- Finding 1: What was tested: whether the effect of `others' alleged opinions about climate change` on `willingness to discuss climate change` was moderated by `personal opinions about climate change`. What was found: the effect was moderated by participants' own concern; participants were more willing to discuss the topic when they were led to believe others would share their opinion than when they were led to believe they would not, and the effect was in the opposite direction for participants with opposing opinions. Effect status: supported.
- Finding 2: What was tested: whether the interaction between `others' alleged opinions about climate change` and `personal opinions about climate change` predicted anticipated evaluations and whether `competent` and `warm` mediated the relation with willingness to discuss climate change. What was found: the effect on expectations of appearing `competent` was moderated by participants' own concern, and expectations of appearing `competent`, but not `warm`, mediated the relation between the interaction and willingness to discuss climate change. Effect status: mixed.
- Null findings: being led to believe that `concern` was `increasing` versus `decreasing` did not affect willingness to discuss climate change and did not affect expectations of appearing `competent` or `warm`; the effect on expectations of appearing `warm` was only marginal and was not supported.

## Meaning of Findings

### Study 1
- Finding 1
  - Result: Perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions, but not perceptions of the `American public`, were related to `willingness to discuss climate change`; the clearest reduction was in the `doubtful` group.
  - Meaning: `self-silencing` in Study 1 was tied specifically to misperceiving relevant peers as doubtful, not simply to any inaccurate perception or to a distant reference group.
- Finding 2
  - Result: Perceptions of other undergraduates' opinions were associated with appearing `competent`, but not with appearing `warm`, and only the `competent` pathway mediated willingness to discuss climate change.
  - Meaning: In Study 1, the barrier to discussion was linked more to expecting less respect than to expecting less liking.
- Null findings
  - Result: Perceptions of the `American public` did not predict willingness or anticipated evaluations, and the `disengaged` and `polarized` groups were not statistically less willing to discuss climate change.
  - Meaning: Not all forms of `pluralistic ignorance` had the same consequence; the paper's strongest Study 1 pattern centered on relevant peer opinion and the `doubtful` perception.

### Study 2
- Finding 1
  - Result: The effect of `others' alleged opinions about climate change` on `willingness to discuss climate change` depended on `personal opinions about climate change`.
  - Meaning: In Study 2, experimentally changing perceived `opinion congruence` changed willingness to speak, and the direction of the effect depended on participants' own opinion.
- Finding 2
  - Result: The interaction between `others' alleged opinions about climate change` and `personal opinions about climate change` predicted expectations of appearing `competent`, and the `competent` pathway, but not the `warm` pathway, mediated willingness to discuss climate change.
  - Meaning: Study 2 extends Study 1 by showing that competence expectations, rather than warmth expectations, explain the effect of perceived agreement on discussion across the opinion spectrum.
- Null findings
  - Result: `concern increasing (vs. decreasing)` did not predict willingness to discuss climate change or expectations of appearing `competent` or `warm`, and the `warm` effect was not supported.
  - Meaning: In Study 2, current perceived agreement mattered more than perceived opinion change over time, and `warmth` was not a reliable explanatory pathway.

## Discussion

1. Theoretical contribution
- The paper argues that `pluralistic ignorance` promotes public silence on climate change by reducing `willingness to discuss climate change` when people perceive that relevant others disagree with them.
- Across both studies, the paper's account of `self-silencing` is that people expect to be respected less, that is, to appear less `competent`, rather than to be disliked or appear less `warm`.

2. Practical implications
- One practical implication is that discussion may be increased by correcting `pluralistic ignorance` among the majority who are concerned about climate change and by making the opinions of the relevant audience salient.
- The paper also suggests that resilience in climate change discussion may be facilitated by improving `communication skills` or boosting `efficacy` about discussing climate change.

3. Limitations
- The paper notes that `self-silencing` should be assessed in more detail, because lack of discussion could reflect intentional silence or lack of personal interest.
- It also points to the need for `behavioral measures` rather than relying only on self-report measures and suggests broader demographic testing beyond the present samples.

## Conclusion

1. Main conclusion
- The paper concludes that `pluralistic ignorance` can be a barrier to climate change discussion and that this barrier can be removed by correcting that `pluralistic ignorance`.

### Klaperski-van der Wal (2025) - The complaining but competent confronter: An experimental examination of the social costs and benefits related to interpersonal confrontations in climate change conversations

DOI: `10.1016/j.jenvp.2025.102738`

## Paper Story

The paper starts from the idea that confronting problematic climate-change statements may discourage people because speaking up could carry `social costs`. The authors revisit that claim with three experimental vignette studies and ask whether confronting `climate change disregard` is still evaluated especially negatively, and whether confrontation style and topic morality matter. Across the studies, they find that confrontation does not produce a broad pattern of negative evaluations specific to climate change, although `static` and `impolite` confrontation can hurt `partner impression`. At the same time, confronters can be seen as more `socially responsible` and more `competent`, which leads the authors to argue that the social consequences of confrontation are more nuanced than earlier work suggested.

## Verbatim Abstract
> [!quote]
> Previous evidence has shown that individuals who openly disapprove of a conversation partner’s environmentally
> undesirable behaviour are subject to negative evaluations from observers, commonly referred to as social
> costs. This study examined whether these findings can still be replicated several years later and whether social
> costs are influenced by the perceived morality of a topic.
> We conducted three separate experimental studies with in total N =715 participants. Participants read an
> online vignette in which provocative statements regarding climate change or injustice were either confronted or
> not. ANOVAs and moderation analyses were used to compare the participants’ ratings of the characters in the
> different confrontation conditions and the role of morality.
> The results revealed that, regardless of the conversation topic, confronters were evaluated significantly more
> negatively than non-confronters on only one social cost outcome. For the first time, we also found evidence for
> the emergence of more beneficial evaluations of confronters in the environmental domain. Furthermore, our
> findings partially support the assumption that more social costs arise when a topic is perceived as less moral.
> We conclude that our findings do not support previous conclusions stating that confronting climate change
> disregard is less acceptable than confronting racism. Instead, our results suggest that confronters of undesirable
> behaviour might in general even be perceived as more competent than non-confronters. An important next step
> for future studies is to examine real conversations and actual behavioural consequences.

## Problem

1. Broader problem

- `interpersonal activism` is important because not responding to anti-environmental behaviours can reinforce the misperception that others do not care about the issue, resulting in a negative feedback loop that inhibits social change.
- At the same time, individuals who openly express disapproval can suffer `social costs`.

2. Specific problem in this paper

- The paper addresses whether `confronters of climate change disregard` still face `social costs`, possibly because climate change is not perceived as a moral issue.
- More specifically, the paper aims to replicate Steentjes et al. (2017) to examine whether the conclusions drawn in 2017 can still be supported today or whether new challenges can be identified.

3. Why this problem matters

- If `climate change disregard` confrontation leads to `social costs`, individuals may be less willing to speak up.
- That matters because misperceptions of prevailing pro-environmental norms can lead to `self-silencing` and inhibit social change.

### Study 1

- `Finding 1`
  - `result`: no significant `interaction effect` between `topic` and `confrontation type` was found for the three `social costs` outcomes; only `partner impression` showed a significant main effect of `confrontation condition`, driven by `static confrontation`
  - `meaning`: the emergence of `social costs` was not specific to `climate change disregard` compared to `social justice`; in this study, negative evaluations were limited rather than broad, and they were concentrated in `partner impression`, especially for `static confrontation`
- `Finding 2`
  - `result`: confrontation increased `agreement with the provocative statement` in some cases, but the pattern differed by topic and confrontation type
  - `meaning`: observing confrontation can sometimes increase agreement with the provocative statement, but this was not a simple climate-specific pattern; the apparent backfire effect depended on how the statement was confronted and on the conversation topic
- `Finding 3`
  - `result`: confronters using `dynamic phrasing` or `static phrasing` were evaluated more positively than a non-confronting character on the exploratory expertise-related items
  - `meaning`: confrontation did not only carry possible `social costs`; it could also elicit more positive evaluations on characteristics linked to expertise or awareness
- `Finding 4`
  - `result`: `Moral Self Subscale` moderated confrontation effects for `partner impressions` and `closeness` in the `social justice` condition, but not in the `climate change` condition
  - `meaning`: perceiver characteristics mattered in a topic-specific way; the effect of confrontation depended on participants’ own `moral identity` in `social justice`, but this pattern did not generalize to `climate change`
- `Null findings`
  - `result`: no broad social-cost pattern emerged across `perceived warmth`, `closeness`, and `partner impression`
  - `meaning`: Study 1 does not support the idea that confrontation generally leads to uniform negative evaluations across outcomes

### Study 2

- `Finding 1`
  - `result`: `impolite confrontation` produced more negative `partner impression` ratings, `polite confrontation` increased `perceived warmth`, and `closeness` did not show a significant effect
  - `meaning`: the social consequences of confrontation depended on how the confrontation was delivered; `impoliteness` was linked to clearer `social costs`, whereas `polite confrontation` did not simply reduce evaluations and could even improve `perceived warmth`
- `Finding 2`
  - `result`: both `polite confrontation` and `impolite confrontation` increased `social responsibility` ratings compared to `no confrontation`
  - `meaning`: confrontation could simultaneously generate `social benefits`, suggesting that speaking up may make a person seem more socially responsible even when some negative evaluations also occur
- `Finding 3`
  - `result`: `confrontation condition` did not significantly influence `agreement with the provocative statement`
  - `meaning`: in Study 2, confrontation changed evaluations of the responding character more than participants’ own agreement with the provocative statement
- `Null findings`
  - `result`: no significant effects were found for `closeness`, and several pairwise comparisons were non-significant
  - `meaning`: the effects of confrontation in Study 2 were selective rather than global across all outcomes

### Study 3

- `Finding 1`
  - `result`: no significant `interaction effect` was found between `topic` and `confrontation condition` for the `social costs` analyses; only `partner impression` showed a significant main effect of `confrontation condition`, and characters in the `racial inequality` condition were evaluated more positively than characters in the `climate change` condition
  - `meaning`: the confrontation effects did not differ by conversation topic in the predicted way; again, `social costs` were limited rather than broad, while the `climate change` topic itself was associated with generally less positive evaluations
- `Finding 2`
  - `result`: `polite confrontation` and `impolite confrontation` increased `competence` ratings compared to `no confrontation`, irrespective of topic
  - `meaning`: one robust positive pattern in Study 3 was that confronters were seen as more `competent`, suggesting that confrontation can communicate competence even when some `social costs` also emerge
- `Finding 3`
  - `result`: `confrontation type` did not affect `agreement with the provocative statement`, but participants in the `climate change` condition agreed more with the provocative statement than participants in the `racial inequality` condition

- `Finding 4`
  - `result`: `racial inequality` was perceived as more moral than `climate change`
  - `meaning`: the paper’s assumption that the two topics differ in `perceived morality` was supported, which provides a basis for examining whether `morality` is related to confrontation effects
- `Finding 5`
  - `result`: `perceived morality` and `moral identity` were related to confrontation effects, but only for selected outcomes and in different ways across the two topics
  - `meaning`: the role of `morality` was conditional rather than general; it shaped some confrontation evaluations, but not all of them and not with one uniform pattern across topics
- `Finding 6`
  - `result`: the ANOVA on `seriousness` showed no topic or confrontation differences, but exploratory moderation analyses showed that higher `seriousness` was related to fewer `social costs` on `partner impression` and higher `competence` ratings for confronters
  - `meaning`: `seriousness` did not distinguish the topics in a simple mean-level way, but it may still matter for how confronters are evaluated when people see the issue as more serious
- `Null findings`
  - `result`: no broad `topic x confrontation` pattern emerged across the main outcomes, and several outcomes showed no significant confrontation effects
  - `meaning`: Study 3 does not support a general claim that confrontation effects differ across topics on all outcomes; the effects remained selective and outcome-specific

## Discussion

### Theoretical contribution

- The paper contributes a replication-based update to Steentjes et al. (2017) by showing that the earlier conclusion about the special unacceptableness of confronting `climate change disregard` was not reproduced.
- A central theoretical contribution is that the literature should not focus only on `social costs`; confrontations can also produce `social benefits`, especially on `social responsibility` and `competence`.
- The authors argue that differences in `perceived morality` alone do not sufficiently explain confrontation effects, because the predicted topic-by-confrontation interactions were not found.
- They further suggest that both `perceived morality` and `seriousness` may shape confrontation evaluations in more conditional ways, and connect this to broader ideas about changing norms and the `dynamic model of moralized social change`.

### Practical implications

- The authors suggest that individuals should not fear being perceived as `incompetent` when confronting `counter-normative statements`, because confronters were often seen as more competent.
- They argue that fewer negative reactions are likely when provocative statements are confronted in a `non-threatening`, `dynamic`, or more `polite` way.
- They explicitly refer to avoiding `H.A.T.E.` (`hostility, aggressiveness, threat, and extremity`) and to using more constructive forms of confrontation.
- They suggest that future work and practice should focus more on `polite and caring enquiry in daily conversations`, and on reducing fears related to voicing one’s opinion about climate change.
- They also argue that making `pro-environmental norms` visible through open expression and observable behaviour may be more promising than only informing people about high levels of concern.

### Limitations

- The vignette situations may not reflect typical climate change conversations, because they involve relatively extreme provocative situations.
- The online vignette design cannot recreate real-life interactional context, including `nonverbal communication cues`, tone of voice, and actual behavioural consequences.
- The findings therefore cannot be generalized directly to real-life encounters, and the authors call for field studies and behavioural measures.
- The choice of outcome variables is a limitation: some `social costs` may exist on outcomes not examined here, and it remains unclear what exactly the `partner impression scale` measures.
- The samples were mainly European, and the first two studies overrepresented women and participants associated with a university, which may bias climate-related responses.

## Conclusion

- Across the three studies, the authors did not find evidence that `confronters of climate change disregard` face higher `social costs` than confronters of statements promoting inequality.
- The most consistent `social cost` pattern was more negative `partner impression` ratings for `static` and `impolite` confrontations, while broader costs on `warmth` and `closeness` did not emerge consistently.
- The authors emphasize that confrontations also yielded `social benefits`: confronters were seen as more `socially responsible` and more `competent`.
- They conclude that the effects of confrontation are more nuanced than assumed in earlier work and may reflect changing societal norms around climate change.

### Steentjes (2017) - The norms associated with climate change: Understanding social norms through acts of interpersonal activism

DOI: `10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2017.01.008`

## Paper Story

The paper starts from the idea that `social norms` matter for climate change, but that research says less about how such norms actually develop in everyday life. Steentjes and colleagues treat `interpersonal confrontation` as one possible route through which norms become visible and contested. They compare confronting `climate change disregard` with confronting `racism` and find that climate confrontation carries social costs, especially in perceptions of `warmth` and `closeness`. In the second study, they show that these costs are tied partly to `perceived morality`: climate change is not treated as sufficiently moral to justify confrontation in the way racism is. The paper concludes that wider constructions of `(im)morality` around climate change shape interpersonal responses in ways that matter for `social change`.

## Verbatim Abstract
> [!quote]
> A growing body of research points to the role social norms may play in both maintaining carbon intensive lifestyles and soliciting changes towards more sustainable ways of living. However, despite highlighting the importance of pro-environmental social norms, such literature has said far less about the processes by which such norms might develop. We present a new approach to conceptualising social norms that focuses on understanding their dynamics within social interaction, by positioning interpersonal confrontation as a potential mechanism of change. We examine the normative dynamics of environmentalism by comparing the costs of interpersonally confronting climate change disregard with those associated with confronting racism. In two experimental studies, we presented participants with scenarios describing a person confronting (versus not confronting) contentious comments in each domain. We identified social costs to interpersonal confrontation of climate change disregard but not racism, as indicated by reduced ratings of perceived warmth of and closeness to the confronter (Study 1), and this effect was mediated by the perceived morality of the issue in question (Study 2). Our findings highlight how wider social constructions of (im)morality around climate change impact upon social interactions in ways that have important implications for processes of social (and ultimately environmental) change.

### Meijers (2014) - Affirming belief in scientific progress reduces environmentally friendly behaviour

DOI: `10.1002/ejsp.2009`

## Paper Story
The paper starts from the paradox that people often know environmental problems are serious yet still fail to behave in environmentally friendly ways. Meijers and Rutjens argue that one reason may be the way science is portrayed in the media: when scientific progress is overstated, people may feel that science will solve environmental problems for them. Drawing on compensatory control theory, the authors propose that belief in scientific progress functions as an external source of order, reducing the need to exert personal control through environmentally friendly behavior. Across four experiments, they show that affirming scientific progress lowers disorder perceptions and reduces pro-environmental attitudes, intentions, and some behavioral choices, whereas questioning scientific progress can have the opposite effect.

## Verbatim Abstract
> [!quote]
> Many people are reluctant to behave in environmentally friendly ways. Simultaneously, communication that questions (vs affirms) scientific progress leads to lower perceptions of order and consequential increases in environmentally friendly behaviour. These findings show that when the aim is to promote environmentally friendly attitudes and behaviour, it helps to not overstate scientific progress. Copyright © 2014 John Wiley & Sons, Ltd.

### Basol (2020) - Good News about Bad News: Gamified Inoculation Boosts Confidence and Cognitive Immunity Against Fake News

DOI: `10.5334/joc.91`

## Paper Story
The paper starts from the problem that online misinformation is difficult to correct once it has spread and lodged itself in memory. Basol, Roozenbeek, and van der Linden argue that a better strategy may be `prebunking`: building resistance before exposure through psychological inoculation. They test a gamified inoculation intervention, `Bad News`, in which participants learn about six common misinformation techniques by playing the role of a fake-news producer in a simulated environment. The study replicates and extends earlier work by adding a randomized control group, more test items, and measures of confidence. The results show that playing `Bad News` reduces the perceived reliability of fake headlines and increases confidence in correct judgments, supporting the idea of a broad-spectrum inoculation against misinformation strategies.

> a medical analogy: by pre-emptively exposing people to weakened doses of misinformation

> news game in which players learn about six common misinformation techniques. We present
> a replication and extension into the effectiveness of Bad News as an anti-misinformation
> intervention. We address three shortcomings identified in the original study: the lack of a
> control group, the relatively low number of test items, and the absence of attitudinal certainty
> measurements. Using a 2 (treatment vs. control) × 2 (pre vs. post) mixed design (N = 196) we

### Fransen (2023) - Sixty Years Later: A Replication Study of McGuire’s First Inoculation Experiment

DOI: `10.1027/1864-1105/a000396`

## Paper Story
The paper starts from the historical importance of McGuire and Papageorgis’s first inoculation experiment, which introduced the idea that resistance to persuasion can be built through pre-exposure to weakened counterarguments. Fransen and colleagues argue that although inoculation theory has been highly influential, its core propositions have not been tested under modern standards with the same rigor. They therefore conduct a high-powered registered-report replication of the original study, comparing an inoculation treatment with a supportive defense treatment and a no-treatment control. The results largely reproduce the classic pattern: inoculation leads to less belief change after a later strong persuasive attack, especially when the two tested truisms are analyzed together.

## Verbatim Abstract
> [!quote]
> Inoculation theory was introduced 60 years ago, after McGuire and Papageorgis (1961) published their first study on how resistance to persuasion can be induced. They demonstrated that people who are pre-exposed to weakened arguments against an attitude or position they currently hold (i.e., inoculated) are less affected by a subsequent strong counter-attitudinal message than people who are pre-exposed to arguments consistent with their attitude (i.e., supportive defense treatment) or to no arguments. Although these results significantly impacted both science and practice on a general level, rigid tests of the key theoretical propositions are lacking. We conducted a highly powered replication study (N = 679) and found that an inoculation treatment is more effective in increasing resistance toward persuasion compared to a supportive defense treatment and a no-treatment control condition. Our results were mostly consistent with McGuire and Papageorgis’s original work.

| Term | Very short definition in this paper |
| --- | --- |
| inoculation theory | The theory that resistance to persuasion can be induced by pre-exposure to weakened counterarguments plus refutations. |
| resistance to persuasion | Reduced attitude change after exposure to a later strong persuasive attack. |
| persuasive attack | A strong counter-attitudinal message intended to change a person’s belief. |
| cultural truism | A widely shared belief whose validity is generally unquestioned within a social environment. |
| threat | Awareness that one’s belief is vulnerable to attack, which motivates defensive processing. |
| refutational pre-emption | The combination of presenting weak counterarguments and refuting them in advance. |

| Term | Very short definition in this paper |
| --- | --- |
| inoculation treatment | A treatment exposing participants to weak counterarguments against a truism and then refuting them. |
| supportive treatment | A one-sided treatment offering only arguments in favor of the truism. |
| no-treatment control | A condition in which participants received no pre-treatment before the later persuasive attack. |
| T0 | The belief measurement taken directly after treatment and before the strong attack message. |
| T1 | The belief measurement taken after exposure to the strong attack message. |
| belief score | Participants’ rating of how true they think a truism is on a 15-point scale. |

## Problem
- Broader problem: People are increasingly confronted with persuasive messages, misinformation, and fake news.
- Broader problem: Understanding how resistance to persuasion can be induced remains highly relevant in this media environment.
- Specific problem in this paper: The paper addresses whether the core theoretical propositions of inoculation theory still hold under a rigorous modern replication of McGuire and Papageorgis’s first experiment.
- Specific problem in this paper: It specifically focuses on whether inoculation produces more resistance to a later persuasive attack than a supportive defense treatment or no treatment.
- Why this problem matters: Inoculation theory has had major influence on research and practice, but the original key propositions have not been tested as rigorously as current standards require.
- Why this problem matters: A solid replication matters because much later research and many practical interventions build on these assumptions.

## Research Questions

### Overall research question(s)
- Can resistance to persuasion be induced through inoculation, as proposed by McGuire and Papageorgis?
- Is an inoculation treatment more effective than a supportive defense treatment in increasing resistance to a later persuasive attack?
- Is an inoculation treatment more effective than a no-treatment control condition in increasing resistance to a later persuasive attack?

### Study-specific research question(s)
- After exposure to a strong counter-attitudinal message, do participants in the inoculation condition show less belief change than participants in the supportive condition?
- After exposure to a strong counter-attitudinal message, do participants in the inoculation condition show less belief change than participants in the no-treatment control condition?
- Do these patterns hold when beliefs are tested on both an original truism about antibiotics and a contemporary truism about investing in education?

## Hypotheses

### Overall hypotheses
- An inoculation treatment will result in more resistance, that is, less attitude change after the later strong counter-attitudinal message, than a supportive treatment.
- An inoculation treatment will result in more resistance, that is, less attitude change after the later strong counter-attitudinal message, than a no-treatment control condition.

### Study-specific hypotheses
- `H1`: An inoculation treatment results in more resistance (`less attitude change`) to the attacking information than a supportive treatment.
- `H2`: An inoculation treatment results in more resistance (`less attitude change`) to the attacking information than a no-treatment control condition.

## Study Setup

### Single study
- Participants: `1,331` students started `T0`. After duplicate participation, low-belief exclusions (`belief score < 10`), missed `T1`, timing failures, and attention-check exclusions, the final included sample was `N = 679`.
- Final condition sizes: inoculation `n = 233`, supportive `n = 234`, control `n = 212`.
- Design: `3 (treatment: inoculation vs. supportive vs. control) x 2 (time: T0 vs. T1)` mixed design, with treatment between-subjects and time within-subjects.
- Topics used: Two truisms were tested in the same treatment condition for each participant: `The effects of antibiotics have, almost without exception, been of great importance to mankind` and `It is of great importance to invest in education`.
- Manipulations/measures: Inoculation condition participants read essays containing weak counterarguments against the truisms plus refutations. Supportive condition participants read one-sided essays supporting the truisms. Control participants received no treatment. Beliefs were measured on a 15-point scale at `T0` and again at `T1` after strong attack messages.
- Timing and attention checks: `T1` occurred `2` to `7` days after `T0`. After the attack messages at `T1`, participants answered content questions; failing `2` of `3` led to exclusion.
- What this study tests: Whether inoculation produces more resistance to a later persuasive attack than a supportive defense treatment and a no-treatment control condition.
- Planned comparison logic: `H1` compares inoculation versus supportive, and `H2` compares inoculation versus control.

## Findings

### Single study: combined truism analysis
- Belief scores dropped from `T0` to `T1` across conditions after the strong attack message. Supported.
- The inoculation treatment produced less belief change than the supportive treatment when the two truisms were averaged. Supported.
- The inoculation treatment also produced less belief change than the no-treatment control condition when the two truisms were averaged. Supported.
- This means both preregistered hypotheses were confirmed in the main combined analysis. Supported.

### Exploratory topic-specific findings
- There was a significant three-way interaction of treatment, time, and topic, meaning the treatment effects differed across the two truisms. Supported.
- For `antibiotics`, inoculation produced less belief change than the supportive treatment. Supported.
- For `antibiotics`, inoculation did not significantly differ from the control condition. Not supported for `H2`.
- For `education`, inoculation produced less belief change than the supportive treatment. Supported.
- For `education`, inoculation also produced less belief change than the control condition. Supported.

### Additional findings
- Contrary to expectation, belief scores at `T0` already differed somewhat between conditions, especially between inoculation and supportive treatment. Supported descriptive finding.
- The replicated effect size for inoculation versus supportive treatment was larger than the effect size reported in the earlier meta-analysis.
- The replicated effect size for inoculation versus control was close to the effect size reported in the earlier meta-analysis, even though it did not match the authors’ conservative estimate from the original study.

### Null findings
- In the topic-specific exploratory analyses, `H2` was not confirmed for the antibiotics truism.
- No significant `T0` difference was observed between inoculation and control or between supportive and control, although inoculation and supportive differed.

## Meaning of Findings

### Single study
- The main result means inoculation still works as a resistance-building strategy under a modern, higher-powered replication.
- Pre-exposure to weakened counterarguments plus refutations appears to help people defend their beliefs better against later strong attacks than either supportive arguments or no treatment.
- The strong drop from `T0` to `T1` across all conditions shows that persuasive attacks can substantially weaken even highly endorsed beliefs, which makes resistance-building practically important.
- The topic-specific divergence suggests inoculation effects are not completely uniform across issues; the antibiotics truism may have been harder to replicate cleanly because the topic and original materials were dated or less clearly shared as a truism today.
- The `T0` differences across conditions indicate that the treatment itself can already influence belief scores, which strengthens the argument for using repeated measurement rather than only a post-attack outcome.

### Broader implication
- The paper suggests that inoculation theory remains robust, but also that cultural truisms may be less stable or less universally shared today than in McGuire’s original context.
- It also implies that interventions derived from inoculation theory differ from more general media-literacy or prebunking approaches because they work by strengthening belief on a specific topic, not only by teaching people to recognize manipulation.

## Discussion

### Main conclusion
- The paper concludes that this was a largely successful replication of McGuire and Papageorgis’s original inoculation experiment.
- When the two truisms were analyzed together, the inoculation treatment produced more resistance to persuasion than both the supportive treatment and the no-treatment control condition.
- The authors therefore conclude that the core assumptions of inoculation theory remain robust after more than 60 years.

### Theoretical contribution
- The study provides a modern, higher-powered test of the original theoretical propositions of inoculation theory.
- It shows the importance of measuring beliefs at multiple time points, because treatment messages can already affect belief scores before the later attack.
- The paper also argues that topic-specific inoculation differs from more general media-literacy or prebunking approaches, because inoculation strengthens a person’s position on a specific issue rather than only helping them recognize manipulation.

### Practical implications
- Strong persuasive attacks can substantially reduce belief in widely endorsed propositions, which makes resistance-building relevant in today’s misinformation environment.
- Inoculation treatments may help protect currently held beliefs against misinformation and disinformation.
- The authors suggest that modern interventions inspired by inoculation theory should preserve the topic-specific strengthening component, rather than relying only on broad warnings about manipulation.

### Limitations
- The replication was only partly successful at the topic level: for the antibiotics truism, inoculation did not outperform the control condition.
- Many participants had lower initial belief scores than expected, suggesting that some classic truisms may no longer function as strong cultural truisms today.
- The strong dropout and exclusion flow reduced the final sample below the original power target.
- The antibiotics materials may have been outdated, which could have weakened their credibility.
- The authors recommend future studies with three measurements: before treatment, after treatment, and after attack, to cleanly separate direct treatment effects from resistance effects.

## Conclusion
- The paper’s main claim is that inoculation still increases resistance to persuasion relative to supportive defense and no treatment under a modern replication.
- At the same time, the results show that such effects can vary by topic and that the status of cultural truisms may shift over time.
- The practical takeaway is that inoculation theory remains a solid foundation for resistance-building interventions, but future work should test them with contemporary topics and stronger measurement designs.
